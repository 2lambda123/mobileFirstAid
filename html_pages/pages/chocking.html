CHOCKING

Choking occurs when a foreign object lodges in the throat or windpipe, blocking the flow of air. In adults, a piece of food often is the culprit most of the time. Young children often swallow small objects. Because choking cuts off oxygen to the brain, administer first aid as quickly as possible. The universal sign for choking is hands clutched to the throat. If the person doesn't give the signal, look for these indications such as Inability to talk, Difficulty in breathing or noisy breathing, Squeaky sounds when trying to breathe, Cough, which may either be weak or forceful, Skin, lips and nails turning blue or dusky, Skin that is flushed, then turns pale or bluish in color, Loss of consciousness

Get Help:

  If the choking individual is conscious, encouraging them to calm and continue coughing freely, it may be easier to take air through the nose to breathe in and refill the lungs.

  Stand to the side and just behind a choking adult.

  For a child, kneel down behind.

  Place one arm across the person's chest for support.

  Deliver five separate back blows between the person's shoulder blades with the heel of your hand

  Perform five abdominal thrusts

  Alternate between 5 blows and 5 thrusts

  Take the victim to the health centre immediately.
